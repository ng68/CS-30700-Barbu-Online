New to the game of Barbu? That's okay. Our goal is to help you learn the basic rules and the different strategies that you can use to become an expert in no time!

What is Barbu?

Barbu is a four-player card game that uses a standard 52 playing card deck. That is the 4 suits - Hearts, Diamonds, Clubs, and Spades each with numbers 2 - 10 with a Jack, Queen, King, and Ace.

What is Barbu?

The game is composed of 7 subgames, which will be described in-depth later. Each round, the dealer selects which of the subgames they want to play. The dealer cannot select this game again for the rest of the game. The game ends when each of the four players has selected each of the 7 subgames; thus, there are 28 rounds in total. At the end of the game, the scores for the players will sum up to zero.

Background Information

6 of the 7 subgames are trick-taking games. The structure of these games is as follows:

  • Some player is chosen to lead. They play the first card of the trick.
  • The player to their left is next to play. They generally must play a card of the same suit as the card that was led (this is called “following suit”) if they can. Play continues like this until the last person has played their card.
  • In games without a trump suit, a player wins the trick if they played the highest card in the trick that is of the same suit as the card that was led. In games with a trump suit, cards of that suit “trump”, or take precedence over, cards of different suits, regardless of whether the card is lower.
  • The winner of the trick is said to have “taken” that trick and all the cards in it.
  • The player who won the previous trick leads for the following trick.
The 7 Subgames

As mentioned earlier, there are 7 total subgames of Barbu. They are:

  • Barbu
  • Last Two
  • Losers
  • Queens
  • Hearts
  • Trumps
  • Fan-Tan

General Rules: For each of the trick-taking games, players must follow suit (play a card of the same suit as the card that was led) if they can. Otherwise, they are free to pitch (play a card of a different suit than the card that was led) any card they want. There are no trump suits unless otherwise specified. With the exception of Fan-Tan, the player to the left of the dealer is the leader for the first trick. Aces are high in each subgame.

The 7 Subgames

Barbu

  • Description: Though this is the name of the game as a whole, there is also a subgame called “Barbu.” This is a trick-taking game in which the only objective is to not take the King of Hearts. Players cannot lead a Heart unless that is the only suit they have in their hand.
  • Scoring: The player who takes the King of Hearts loses 20 points.
  • Strategy: If you have the King of Hearts, try to get rid of all your cards in another suit so that you can pitch the King of Hearts early. If you don’t have the King of Hearts, try to avoid taking tricks in which you think someone might pitch the King of Hearts - for example, you probably want to avoid taking the trick when Spades are led for the third time, as whoever has the King of Hearts may have already run out of Spades.
The 7 Subgames

Last Two

  • Description: This is a trick-taking game in which the objective is to not take the last two tricks.
  • Scoring: The player who takes the second-to-last trick loses 10 points. The player who takes the last trick loses 20 points. If the same player takes both tricks, they lose 30 points.
  • Strategy: Save your low cards for the end! The more cards you have in one suit, the more you should want your last cards to be in that suit (since other players will run out of that suit first). Also, try to get off the lead before the last two tricks - it can be dangerous to be on lead towards the end of the game.
The 7 Subgames

Losers

  • Description: This is a trick-taking game in which the goal is to not take tricks.
  • Scoring: Players lose 2 points for each trick they take.
  • Strategy: In general, you should play your highest card that won’t take the trick. If you have to take the trick, you should play your highest card.
The 7 Subgames

Queens

  • Description: This is a trick-taking game in which the goal is to not take Queens.
  • Scoring: Players lose 6 points for each Queen they take.
  • Strategy: In general, you should avoid playing Aces or Kings unless it’s the last card of the trick (in this case, it gives you an easy way to get rid of a dangerous card). Use pitching to your advantage - if you don’t have a card of the same suit as the card that was led, pitch one of your Aces, Kings, or Queens.
The 7 Subgames

Hearts

  • Description: This is a trick-taking game in which the goal is to not take Hearts. Unlike the popular card game of the same name, there is no penalty for taking the Queen of Spades, and players cannot “shoot the moon.” Players cannot lead a heart until they have been broken (i.e. someone else has pitched a heart), unless that is the only suit they have.
  • Scoring: Players lose 6 points if they take the Ace of Hearts. All other Hearts carry a penalty of 2 points each.
  • Strategy: Try to avoid taking tricks involving Hearts. One or two won’t hurt you, but if the trick has 4 Hearts then it will hurt your score much more. Having a lot of Hearts can actually help you - it gives you more control over who takes Hearts, and you don’t have to worry about people pitching Hearts in tricks you win. Also, keep in mind that the Ace of Hearts has a penalty 3 times worse than a normal Heart.
The 7 Subgames

Trumps

  • Description: This is a trick-taking game in which the goal is to take tricks. As the name suggests, there is a trump suit; this is chosen by the dealer when they select the game.
  • Scoring: Players gain 5 points for each trick they take.
  • Strategy: Win as many tricks as you can! When picking this game, select a trump suit in which you have many cards and several high cards. If you know you’re going to lose a trick, play the lowest card you can. When trumping, play your lowest trump card unless someone else has already played a higher trump.
The 7 Subgames

Fan-Tan

  • Description: This is the only non-trick-taking game. The objective of the game is to play all your cards before the other players. The dealer chooses a start card, e.g. 2 to Ace, and begins the game by playing one of the start cards. For example, if the dealer chooses the start card to be the Jack, then they can begin by playing the Jack of any suit. Each suit will have its own row in which the start card is the first card to be played. From there, players build the row by playing a card adjacent to the cards at the end of the row. For example, if the 7 is chosen as a start card and the 7 of Clubs is played, the next player can either play the 6 of Clubs, 8 of Clubs, or the 7 of any other suit. Players pass if they cannot make a legal play. At the end of the game, each suit will have a full row going from 2 to Ace.
  • Scoring: The first player to play all their cards gains 45 points. The second gains 20 points, the third gains 5 points, and the last loses 5 points.
The 7 Subgames

Fan-Tan

  • Strategy: The main goal in this game is to have control of the board. The best possible position to be in is when you have the only cards that are allowed to be played and no one else can play anything. When you need help to be able to eventually play a card, focus on that first - for example, if the start card is 8 and you have the 7 and the 2 of Spades but nothing in between, you should play the 7 early so that other players will have time to play the cards in between. Conversely, if you just have the 7, you should hold off from playing it until you have to, since you don’t need any help there.
Doubling

After the subgame is chosen, each player will have an opportunity to double. A double is essentially a side bet between two players. If Player A doubles Player B, they will gain extra points if they do better than Player B in that game, and Player B will lose extra points; conversely, if Player B does better, they will gain extra points and Player A will lose extra points.

Doubling

Procedure: Doubling starts with the player to the dealer’s left. In the five negative contracts (Losers, Barbu, Queens, Hearts, and Last Two), the player will have the option to double any of the other players; in the two positive contracts (Trumps and Fan-Tan), the player can only double the dealer or pass. Once they have made their selections, the next player to the left makes theirs. If Player B has already been doubled by Player A before they make their selections, they have the option to redouble, which raises the stakes of the bet. Doubling continues in this manner until reaching the dealer. The dealer cannot double other players, but can redouble any players who have already doubled them. Once the dealer has made their selections, play begins.

Doubling

Scoring: At the end of the game, raw scores are calculated for each player according to the scoring system for the subgame of choice. For each double (say, between Player A and Player B), calculate the difference between their scores. This difference is added to the score of the player who did better and subtracted from the score of the player who did worse. If it was a redouble instead of a double, the difference is doubled.

Doubling

Example: Say that Players A, B, C, and D played a round of Losers and D was the dealer. Let’s say the raw scores are:

  • A: -4
  • B: -10
  • C: -6
  • D: -6

Let’s also say that the doubling went as follows:

  • A doubled B
  • B redoubled A and doubled C
  • C doubled D
Doubling

First, let’s handle the redouble between A and B. A did 6 points better than B, so we add 12 points to A’s score and subtract 12 from B’s score. Now we have A at +8 and B at -22. B also doubled C and did 4 points worse, so we subtract 4 from B’s score and add 4 to C’s score. Now we have A at +8, B at -26, and C at -2. Finally, C doubled D and did just as well, so this double is a wash. The final scores for the round are A at +8, B and -26, C at -2, and D at -6. Notice that the scores for the round still add up to -26.
